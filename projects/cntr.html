Project Description

The goal of this project is to produce a 3D-printed educational model of a centrifugal nuclear thermal rocket (CNTR) engine that can be easily assembled and disassembled. The engine is part of ongoing research supported by NASA for future deep-space propulsion, including potential missions to Mars. NASA provided the necessary tools to educate the team on the operation of the CNTR. Resources, such as CAD models of the engine, were also provided. This project was completed by a team of four students as part of a mechanical engineering capstone project at the University of Rhode Island.

Engine Functionality

A cross-sectional view of the engine is shown in Figure 1. In the figure, the hydrogen propellant path is shown (green arrows). The initially cold hydrogen flows into the inlet to the left of the figure. The hydrogen propellant enters from the left side of the engine and initially flows along the outer region of the vessel, providing cooling. It then travels downward and is redirected upward along the outer surface of the fuel elements. After reaching the top, the flow is directed into the centrifuges through openings in the tapered surface. Centrifugal forces push the hydrogen outward against the inner walls of the fuel elements before it moves inward, where it is heated by the nuclear fuel. The high-temperature propellant then exits through the nozzle on the right.

The gray section is the moderator block, which contains a porous medium of a material yet to be finalized. The material that will most likely be used is beryllium or silicon carbide. The red area represents the liquid uranium, while the orange is a mixture of uranium and hydrogen. This part of the operation of the engine is a major challenge because transporting gas through a liquid medium presents significant engineering challenges. Also, the hydrogen inflow needs to separate the hydrogen from the uranium originally. This inflow would need to be porous to allow the hydrogen to pass, but while preventing uranium from flowing radially outward due to centrifugal forces.

Model Design

The final design of the educational model is shown in Figure 2. A cross-sectional view of the model is shown in Figure 3. The original design was modified from above to make the assembly and disassembly processes easier, particularly for users unfamiliar with the system. SolidWorks was used for the CAD design.

The bill of materials for the full model is given in Table 2. The numbers accurately represent characteristics from the real model in Figure 1. A total of 36 parts are needed for the 3D printed model. An exploded view of all the parts in the model is shown in Figure 4. In this view, the quantities from the table can easily be seen.

There are some changes between the model that was provided and the model that was printed that are worth mentioning. The turbine blades of the centrifuges needed to be thickened significantly to allow for traditional 3D printing. More realistic blades were produced on resin 3D printers, although it wasn’t practical to print the whole model on these printers. Another modification was the addition of a nozzle to improve visual resemblance to a spacecraft. Lastly, The cylindrical symmetry of the model was leveraged to create easy mating of the parts. A simple slide, 90-degree twist, and lock of the larger components was implemented. Figure 5 shows a view of this locking mechanism.

Model 3D Printing

The fully printed and assembled model is shown in Figure 6. A cutaway view of the model, useful for educational purposes, is shown in Figure 7. Each centrifuge was painted so that the four main components could be identified. These components are the centrifuge body (brown), turbine blades (silver), redirection disk (white), and bearing (black). An image of the painted centrifuges is shown.

A breakdown of time and filament requirements to complete this project for both the full model and cross-sectional model is given in Tables 3 and 4, respectively. The full project took over 600 hours of print time and 5.6 kg of filament to complete. The cost of all the filament was $140.

Some additional specifications of the 3D printing process are shown in Table 5. All dimensions of the engine were scaled down by a factor of 4.4, which results in a volume scale factor of 85. The small nozzle diameter and layer heights explain the long print times and high-resolution of the print quality.
